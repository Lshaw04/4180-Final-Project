ECE 4180-Luke Shaw-Painting/Drawing Robot
My project is an autonomous painting robot designed to bridge the gap between digital sketching and physical large-scale art. The core concept involves using a 5-way navigation switch to create a drawing on a uLCD screen. Because a navigation switch alone only allows for continuous paths, I integrated a separate pushbutton to act as a "pen lift" feature, allowing the user to create disconnected lines and complex shapes. Once a drawing is finalized, the user presses the center button of the navigation switch to transmit the coordinates from the controller ESP32 to a receiver ESP32 on the robot via the ESP-NOW protocol. The receiving microcontroller then translates this drawing logic into pulse-width modulation (PWM) signals to drive the chassis, while a servo motor manages the physical pen-up and pen-down mechanics.

To power the system, I utilized an L298N H-bridge motor driver. I specifically chose this older model because of its integrated voltage regulator; it can handle a high-voltage input from a 3S LiPo battery while simultaneously outputting a steady 5V to power the ESP32 logic. During development, I encountered a significant power sequencing issue: when the servo moved, its high current draw caused a voltage sag that would brown out the ESP32. To resolve this, I decoupled the power supplies, using a dedicated 3-cell AA battery pack from the lab to power the servo while the LiPo handled the high-torque drive motors.

The most challenging technical hurdle was achieving precise steering. Budget TT motors rarely spin at the same rate due to friction, voltage variances, and uneven weight distribution. I initially tried to manually offset the PWM signals, but I discovered a significant "dead zone" where small increments had no effect, while larger increments caused the robot to veer wildly. My solution was to implement closed-loop feedback using magnetic wheel encoders with 8-pole polarity. I experimented with two logic styles: a "leader-follower" method where one wheel constantly tried to catch up, and a "symmetric see-saw" method where both wheels adjusted simultaneously based on the real-time error delta. I ultimately chose the symmetric method for its superior stability.

This project functions essentially as a CNC plotting machine with an infinite workspace, making it ideal for creating murals or large art pieces that exceed the bounds of a traditional frame. If I were to improve the design with more time, I would first address the weight distribution; with both battery packs located in the rear, the front caster would lift off the ground during acceleration. Moving the wheel base forward or redistributing the batteries would fix this. Additionally, I would swap the TT motors for stepper motors. In hindsight, the inherent precision of steppers would eliminate the "dead zone" issues and make software-based trajectory adjustments significantly less complex.
